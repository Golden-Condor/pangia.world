Insights framework

Asheville environmental baseline

This page is the working framework for a local resource guide that helps homeowners make sense of Asheville and Western North Carolina environmental history. It is meant to organize the questions first: what happened, where it matters, what pathways are relevant now, and what still needs stronger evidence.

In build

Pangia is treating this as a structured research layer. The page is live now so the taxonomy is visible, but the detailed issue coverage will grow as individual briefs are sourced and tightened.

What this page does

Organizes local issues into categories homeowners can actually use: water system performance, industrial legacy, watershed context, and house-specific testing implications.

Baseline categories

  • Municipal water and distribution questions
  • Historic industrial and downstream issues
  • Well, spring, and watershed context
  • Flooding, sediment, and site-specific concerns

1. Why this should exist

Local environmental information is fragmented.

Most homeowners do not need more headlines. They need a way to understand whether a local issue is historical context, a real present-day exposure concern, or simply something that should inform how they interpret a lab result. In Asheville and Western North Carolina, those threads are often scattered across public records, utility reports, reporting archives, and agency documents.

2. What Pangia wants this section to answer

Not just what happened, but what still matters at the household level.

Each issue brief should eventually answer the same set of practical questions:

  1. What is the underlying issue or historical event?
  2. Which geography or watershed does it touch?
  3. Which exposure pathways are relevant now: water, soil, dust, sediment, or something else?
  4. What is known from public records, and what remains uncertain?
  5. When does a homeowner actually need testing instead of more reading?

3. Current issue groups

The first version should stay narrow and useful.

The strongest starting set is already clear: Asheville municipal water interpretation, coal ash and chromium questions, the Canton paper mill legacy, DuPont-related legacy questions, and any watershed or flooding issues that change how households think about their water source.

That is enough to make this section useful without turning it into an unstructured archive.

4. How a homeowner should use it

Read local context first. Test second.

These briefs are meant to help people narrow the right question before spending money or assuming the worst. The goal is to move from local context to site-specific action: a municipal-water interpretation, a certified panel, a well test, or a broader Diagnostic conversation.
